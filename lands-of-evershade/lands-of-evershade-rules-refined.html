PART 1: CORE CONCEPTS & GAME OVERVIEW

Five thousand miles under the failing disc of Luada.
Dozens of kingdoms, tribes, and races, all knowing the world will end,
and all too conflicted to act.
Cities sprawling around the rays of light that bring doom, but also power.
Mysteries of the past, waiting to be uncovered.
Whoever you are and whatever your story is, one thing is certain.
In Evershade, adventure will find you.

WHAT IS LANDS OF EVERSHADE?

Lands of Evershade is a campaign, cooperative, narrative board game for 1-5
players, inspired by classic tabletop role playing games.
To begin their journey, each player uses the Player's Handbook to create
a unique character, selecting from a wide range of races, professions, ori-
gins, paths, and other characteristics.
Then, a Party of these Characters sets upon one of several available Ad-
ventures that allow the Characters to make their way through gripping
stories, fight Enemies on tactical maps, and explore various areas of Ever-
shade. In the process, the Characters advance and gather better equipment
to handle even bigger challenges.
Each Adventure takes from 12-18 hours, and is most often divided into 3-6
hour Chapters, each of which with two 1-3 hour Acts.
Once a Character completes their final Adventure (Expert level Adventure),
they can either embark on one of the deadly, dangerous Epic scenarios or
retire, making way for a new generation of adventurers.

CORE CONCEPTS GOLDEN RULES

Rounding values. Each time you have to count fractional values, round them up to whole numbers.

Component limits. Most components in the game have a limited supply. If you are to draw, use, or place a component and all copies of it are already in play, ignore the effect. Universal markers, HP dials, and dice are the only exception. In rare cases, when you have run out of these components, find and use a substitute.

Order of effect resolution. In the rare case where you must resolve multiple game effects at the same time, players may choose the order of resolution.

Detailed effects overwrite basic rules. If you find effects on any component that in some way contradict the rules from this rulebook, the detailed effect takes precedence.

CHARACTERS

Each player controls one Character during the game. A Character is described with their Attributes, race, profession and keywords. They determine if your Character is a strong but asocial alf with a troubled past, a dextrous and dashing blank from a port town, a weak but magic-profi-cient shrewck, and many more. Each Character may also be equipped with a multitude of different Items to aid them in their endeavours, have Action cards representing their Combat maneuvers and spells, and keep some brill on their person – the currency used in Evershade. All of those components are stored on the Character board and Hotbar. The excess that cannot fit into the slots is placed in the Pouch – the personal storage of a Character.

PARTY

When adventurers join forces, they form a Party. A Party consists of a minimum of two Characters and potentially some Companions. If playing solo, the Party will consist of a player Character traveling with a Partner – a stronger Companion to help them. The Party fights and explores together, and makes their decisions about the story as a team. This does not exclude the possibility of heated debates whether to help a wounded merchant on the road or rob them and leave them to die.

ADVENTURES

In Lands of Evershade the Party may embark on three types of Adventures – Starting level for beginners, Advanced level for experienced Characters, and Expert level serving as a conclusion of the journey for veteran Characters. During Adventures the Party will live through the story, traverse the land, fight their way through encounters, explore their backstories, Camp, gather Experience, and Advance their skills. After an Expert level Adventure the Character may embark on the last, deadly Epic scenario or retire. While playing an Adventure, keep in mind that you should use the components from the base game, as well as the components from the current Adventure – this includes the Storybook, the Secret Sheets and other components marked with an appropriate code (e.g. 'ItW' for the 'Into the White-well' Adventure).

CHAPTERS AND ACTS

Each Adventure is divided into three smaller parts called Chapters. Chapters mark the important story parts of an Adventure. Chapters are further divided into Acts. Each Act is a perfect fragment of the Adventure to play during a single game session and may take from 1 to 3 hours. This depends on how thoroughly the players decide to explore, whether they have Companions in their Camp to interact with, and what additional interactions they play through as a part of their Personal Secrets. After each Act, you will have an opportunity to save the game.

PLAYING THE GAME

In this section, you will find an overview on how to play the game. This will come in the form of a brief description of all main systems and parts of the Adventure you may encounter. The introduction here should be sufficient to begin playing, but if you need more detailed descriptions with examples, they might be found in "Part 3: Characters" and "Part 4: Detailed rules".

The game is divided into two parts. The first – Exploration – is facilitated with the Storybook and Secret Sheets. This part includes traversing wilderness, exploring cities, tackling dungeons, solving puzzles, and engaging in social interactions. During Exploration, the game always instructs you what to do next. It might be reading an entry in the Storybook or taking an Action from a Secret Sheet.

The second part is Combat. It is played on Secret Sheets with grid-based tactical maps as turn-based encounters. In Combat, Characters and Enemies perform Activations in a specified order.

SECRET SHEETS

Secret Sheets are numbered boards used to represent the environment and various scenes you will encounter during an Adventure. The Storybook instructs you when to place a new Sheet or discard the one you no longer need. The Story Tracker has room for two Secret Sheets – one on the left and the second on the right side. All the unused Secret Sheets should be placed under the Cover Sheet to prevent accidental spoilers. Each Secret Sheet has its special rules and Actions applying to this part of Exploration or Combat.

For details on Secret Sheets, go to page 18.

COMBAT

Combats are played as turn-based encounters on a tactical grid. During Combat each participant – Party Members or Enemies – will resolve their Activation. To determine the order of Activation, you will draw tokens from the Initiative bag.

During their Activation, Enemies will resolve their Actions written on their Activation cards. Characters during Activation may spend 2 Actions on various activities such as moving around the arena, resolving Action cards or performing special abilities.

For details on Combat, go to page 20.

IMPORTANT ICON – CHARACTER NUMBER
The icon icon represents the number of Characters present in the game, and should be read as a number (e.g. 3 in a 3 Character game). There is also a variant of icon, which represents half of the Character Number (e.g. 1.5 rounded up to 2 in a 3 Character game).

STORYBOOK

The story in an Adventure is presented in a Storybook. It contains numbered entries with story descriptions and choices. When navigating the Storybook, the entries will redirect you to other entries (e.g. "Go to 100"), instruct you to mark your decisions in the Story Tracker, progress Time and much more. When making a decision, the Party should agree on one option. If they cannot come to a conclusion, the Character with the Decision token makes the final call. Some redirections or choices may require you to have proper keywords. Entries may also contain Rolls.

For details on Storybook navigation go to page 15.

ROLLS

Many Actions in this game are resolved with Rolls – you will roll twelve-sided dice (D12) to determine if your Action is successful or not. Each time you are instructed to perform a Roll, choose one Character to do that. Take a number of D12s according to the Attribute (ATT) of the Roll (STR, AGI, INT or CHA), and include modifiers – most often additional dice for having proper keywords. Other Characters may Support the Roll by rolling their own dice, but only if they meet the requirements of the modifiers. Each Roll has a set Difficulty shown by the icon icon. Each die with a value equal to, or higher than the Difficulty counts as 1 Success. A die with a value of 12 counts as 2 Successes. You need to achieve as many Successes as described in the outcome of the Roll for it to be successful.

During Rolls, you may spend Fate icon to reroll dice. If you fail a Roll or roll a 1 on a die, you will gain Fate icon.

For details on performing Rolls and Supporting them, go to page 16.

PART 2: SETUP, SAVING AND RESTORING THE GAME BEGIN YOUR FIRST ADVENTURE (SETUP STEP 1 OF 4)

This part is written for those who have never played Lands of Evershade before. It will guide you through your first setup and take you to the Into the Whitewell Adventure that contains helpful Tutorials and introduces game rules in a gradual manner.

Important: Creating each new Lands of Evershade Character involves choosing from a long list of races, professions, origins, paths and Companions. That’s why all steps between here and the final setup step may take up to an hour per player. In larger groups, consider devoting the first game session to building your Party: perform all the setup steps and return to the game after you’re rested and ready to play!

a) Prepare core components.

  • Make sure that all the components are in the places indicated by the unboxing instructions found in the Start Here document.
    • All types of Item and Character tokens should be in separate compartments.
    • All cards should be sorted by the number in card trays.
  • Prepare a pool of D12s and a set of Divinum dice, or standard RPG dice: D4, D6, D8, D10, D12, and D20.
  • Place the Story Tracker in the center of the table so that there’s a place for at least one Sheet to the left and one to the right of it.
  • Find all three books required to embark on the first Adventure: this Rulebook, the Player’s Handbook, and the Into the Whitewell Storybook. The setup steps will continue throughout all three of them.

b) Add the components of Into the Whitewell.

  • Take all cards and tokens from the Into the Whitewell Adventure box and add them to the game box.
  • Cards, sheets and tokens from Into the Whitewell have the ItW tag next to their number. Do not place components with any other Adventure tags in the box for now.
  • Find all Into the Whitewell Sheets (ItW 1-16) and place them in one stack under the Cover Sheet.
    • Note: The Cover Sheet prevents accidental spoilers. You should not look at these Sheets until the game asks you to place them in the Story Tracker.

c) Start building your Party.

  • Go to Chapter 2 of the Player’s Handbook and follow the Create a Character (Setup Step 2 of 4) instructions to build your first Character.
    • Note: After creating the first Character, the Handbook will guide you further. You will not return to this Rulebook until after the first Act of the starting Adventure.

b) Choose an Adventure. Advanced and Expert Adventures require an experienced Party. If you wish to play with existing, saved Characters, use the list below to find an Adventure of appropriate difficulty:

  • You’ve completed no Adventures → Choose a Starting Adventure
  • You’ve completed a Starting Adventure → Choose an Advanced Adventure
  • You’ve completed an Advanced Adventure → Choose an Expert Adventure

c) Add the chosen Adventure’s components.

  • Take all cards and tokens from this Adventure’s box and add them to the game box.

e) If you have no Characters, go to Chapter 2 of the Player’s Handbook and follow the Create a Character (Setup Step 2 of 4) instructions to build your first Character.

SAVING THE GAME

The game provides a convenient place to save after completing each Act. If you wish to stop playing and save the game in any other place, you can take notes and photos of the current state of the game to be able to restore it later.

To save the game at the end of an Act, resolve all the following steps:

  1. Note down which Act of the Adventure you have completed.
  2. Save each Character in a Character tray: place all cards, Hotbars and Character boards in trays and cover them with a lid. You do not need to disassemble the Character boards - the lids will hold everything securely in place.
  3. Return all other tokens and cards to the component trays.
  4. Place the Story Tracker in the tray and cover it with a lid. Do not remove any marks from the Story Tracker - the lid will hold them securely in place.
  5. Place all components, trays, and pouches in the box.

Note: In the Standard edition of the game, there are no special lids for saving the game. Instead, use the provided Save Sheet to note down the current state of the game.

RESTORING THE GAME

To restore the game, follow the instructions in the "Resuming the Adventure" section at the beginning of the Storybook. This includes:

  1. Restoring all Characters with all their tokens, Items, Action cards, HP, Exp, etc.
  2. Restoring the Story Tracker with all marks and tokens.
  3. Going to the appropriate entry, depending on the Act.

PART 3: CHARACTERS CHARACTER BOARD AND HOTBAR

All information about a Character is stored in the form of tokens and markers on a Character board.
The Hotbar is a complementary board for Action & Reaction cards.

  • A) Hit Points (HP). The endurance and stamina of your Character.
  • B) Basic Attributes: Strength (STR), Agility (AGI), Intellect (INT), Charisma (CHA).
  • C) Divinum (DIV). A power to reshape reality, originating from the Higher Realm.
  • D) Mobility (MOB). Your Character's movement range during Combat.
  • E) Boons & Terror. Positive and negative ongoing effects that affect your Character.
  • F) Character tokens. The collection of keywords representing all major life experiences of your Character.
  • G) Equipment. Equipped Weapons, Armor and ready-to-use Items.
  • H) Storage for brill and Fate icon.
  • I) Advancement trees. You will slot any earned Advancement tokens here to represent choices made during the Character's development.
  • J) Experience. This measures the Exp points gained by the Character and shows how many more you need to gain the next Advancement.
  • K) Hotbar. The space for your Character's prepared Action and Reaction cards. The bottom four spaces will become available after a Character reaches a set number of Advancements.
KEYWORDS

In Lands of Evershade, each Character has multiple keywords that define them. These keywords are often checked by dialogue choices, gameplay options, and various mechanics. The keywords are written in bold italics. Also the names of your profession and race are considered keywords. All the keywords you currently have can be found on:

  1. Tokens in the Character space of your Character board.
  2. Tokens in the Equipment space of your Character board.
  3. Face up Action and Reaction cards currently on the Hotbar.
  4. Your Companion card (except in Combat).
  5. The space your model currently occupies and the Sheet you’re currently playing.

Examples: You have found a Strange Egg item that requires a Light keyword to hatch. You have that keyword and satisfy the requirement in each of the following cases:

  • You have an equipped Sword of Light Item, since its token has the Light keyword.
  • You have a Personal Secret token that has the Light keyword.
  • You stand in a space on the map that has the Light keyword.
  • You have a Companion who has the Light keyword.

You do not have the keyword and do not satisfy the egg’s requirement if:

  • You have the Light keyword on an Item currently in your Pouch (you need to equip it first).
  • The Light keyword is on an Action card that’s currently face down (you need to refresh it first).
  • The Light keyword is in a space adjacent to your Character model (you have to stand in that space first).

Some keyword checks are worded differently on purpose. Instead of checking if you have the keyword, they may check more specific, or broader conditions.

Example: An Action on a tactical grid requires a Ranged keyword anywhere within range 1 of you. This means, if another Character with a Ranged keyword is on the adjacent space, you meet the requirements of that Action, even though you do not have the Ranged keyword yourself.

ATTRIBUTES (ATT)

The following Attributes describe the basic abilities of a Character and define the base number of dice used during Rolls:

  • Strength – STR
  • Agility – AGI
  • Intellect – INT
  • Charisma – CHA

Attributes are marked with appropriate tokens in the Character board and may range from 1 to 6. Attributes may be increased with Advancements.

EXPERIENCE AND ADVANCEMENTS

During an Adventure, the Characters will gain Experience points (Exp). They represent new knowledge and skills that mark the Character’s progress as an adventurer. Exp is tracked on the right side of the Character board. Whenever the marker in the Exp track reaches the current topmost empty space, the Character gains an Advancement! When that happens, resolve the following:

  • Remove the bottom Advancement token from the Exp track and place it in your Pouch.
  • Place the marker in the Exp track on the bottom slot (0). If needed, continue gaining the remaining Exp.

Example: The Character has 0 Exp and no Advancements. The Storybook instructs them to gain 3 Exp. They move the marker on the Exp track by 2 and it reaches the first Advancement token. They remove it from the track, place the Advancement token in the Pouch and reset the Exp track to 0. After that they gain the remaining 1 Exp.

During Camp, the Characters will have an opportunity to Advance if they have any Advancement tokens in their Pouch. The rules for Advancements and contents of each Advancement Tree can be found in the Player’s Handbook.

icon Advancing your Character unlocks new options on the Hotbar and Advancement trees. For example, a Hotbar slot with this icon can be used after at least 1 Advancement token has been allocated.

DIVINUM (DIV)

Divinum is the mysterious substance of gods that can be used to reshape reality. The more Divinum an individual has absorbed, the more powerful an arcane wielder they are. Each Character has a value of DIV on their Character board. The token indicates how many and what type of dice (e.g. D6, D8 or D12+D4) the Character uses when asked to roll for Divinum. Some Rolls in the game will additionally require the use of a Divinum die. In the game, depending on the context, Divinum might be referenced with its name, DIV tag or icons icon for full value or icon for half of the value.

When a Character is instructed to increase their DIV use the following progression:

MOBILITY (MOB)

Mobility defines how quick a Character is and how fast they can run. In Combat, it shows how many spaces the Character may move in a single turn. It is sometimes also checked during Exploration.

EQUIPMENT & INVENTORY

This is the place on the Character board to store equipped items. It consists of 3 spaces for handheld Items, one for headgear, one for chestpiece, one for footwear and 4 for small Items. Additionally, there is a larger slot to keep Fate and brill.

HOTBAR

This additional board shows which Action cards the Character has prepared at the moment. Action cards come in 3 different types (Offensive, Defensive and Support), and each card has to occupy a matching slot – one card per slot.
Action cards may be granted by class Advancements or currently equipped Items.
Additional slots (marked with a lock and a number) are available after reaching a set number of Advancements.

Example: Offensive cards have to be placed in a slot with a matching type. Some slots may hold different card types.

FATE icon/icon

Fate icon represents the forces of destiny that shape the story of your Character. You may pay Fate to increase your chances in Rolls and Combat situations.
Keep any Fate tokens you have gained in your Brill & Fate slot. Once they are used, they return to the component pool. Fate is the most likely component to hit the component limit of 10. If there is not enough Fate in the component pool, you cannot gain more.

USING FATE

Fate tokens can be spent in the following ways. You may use those options multiple times, as long as you have enough icon:

  • During a Roll, pay 1icon to reroll any number of your dice (including DIV dice).
  • Pay Xicon to refresh an Action card (if the card allows for it, the cost and other details are on the card).
  • During Character Activation, pay 3icon to immediately gain 1 additional Action.
GAINING FATE

Many game effects may allow you to gain icon. There are two main ways to gain icon, and they are connected to performing Rolls:
a) You gain 1icon whenever your Character fails their Roll (resolves icon). 

- Only the final outcome of the Roll counts. If you fail, but then pay [icon] or other resources to reroll and achieve a positive outcome, you don’t gain [icon].  
- If a failure has no specific effects, the [icon] outcome may not appear in the Roll. However, failing to achieve a positive outcome still counts as a failure and grants 1[icon].

b) You gain 1icon for each natural 1 on a D12 that remains at the end of the Roll after all the re-rolls and other die modifications. This does not include DIV dice.

ITEMS

Characters acquire Items during the game to power up. Most of the Items have their own keywords. Many of them grant a unique Action card and/or a passive modifier to damage (icon) or armor (icon).
Each Character starts with a unique Club/Staff Sling double sided Item. Each time you equip it, you may choose the side of the token to be active.
Each time you equip an Item, you may choose the side of the token to be active. Some Items can be used as free Actions during Combat (see "Items in Combat" p. 25).

USING ITEMS

Equipment is the set of Items kept in the slots of the Character board. During Exploration, only the Items in the Equipment slots contribute their keywords to the Character. Some Action cards also require specific Items or keywords in your Equipment – losing an Item may turn some Action cards unusable. If an Action card is used by an Item that’s no longer in your Equipment or Pouch, return it to the box. Some Items can be used as free Actions during Combat (see "Items in Combat" p. 25).

POUCH

This is the personal storage of a Character. All Items and Action cards not currently in use, as well as Advancement tokens, are placed in the Pouch. Characters can reequip Items and Action cards from the Pouch during Camp.
All keywords on components in the Pouch are not active and cannot be used. However, if the story checks a requirement of an Item, the requirement is met regardless if it is in the Equipment or Pouch.

BRILL

Brill is the currency used in Lands of Evershade. Each Character has their own brill stored in the inventory space on their Character board. When the Party gains brill, it may be divided between the Characters in any way, unless stated otherwise. When brill has to be spent, the Party may divide the cost however they like, unless stated otherwise.

HP

In Lands of Evershade, HP represents minor injuries, vigor and stamina during Combat. These points reset at each Camp Phase and in some story moments. Characters may also regain HP with heal effects. Maximum HP is marked on the Character board with the HP limiters.
During Exploration, after reaching 0 HP, a Character gains the Injury Condition and 1 Fate, then rolls their DIV die and restores a number of HP equal to the result.
In Combat, when a Character drops to 0 HP, they are Knocked Out or Eliminated (see “Combat” p. 20).

CONDITIONS

Conditions, represented by tokens such as Injury or Trauma, are more serious, lingering afflictions that you have to take care of (for example, in a Camp or by visiting a healer). There are 4 different Conditions: Injury, Trauma, Sunbleached and Dimrot. A Character can never have both Sunbleached and Dimrot Conditions at once – if they would gain one of them while having the other, simply flip the token to the other side. If a Character would gain a second Sunbleached or Dimrot they gain an Injury instead.
If a Character has three Condition tokens and has to gain a fourth, they die (see below).

CHARACTER DEATH

Any time a Character has three tokens in the Condition slots of their Character board, and receive another (fourth) Condition, they die! Death might also be a result of other game effects, for example, Terror.
When a Character dies, resolve all steps below:
a) If this player has the Decision token, pass it clockwise.
b) All other Characters gain 1 Terror icon.
c) Put all the Quest Items in the Pouch of the Character with the Decision token. Put all other Items of the dead Character in Pouches of other Characters, divided in any way players agree on.
d) Share the brull of the Character in any way the players agree on.

e) If the Character has a Companion, give the Companion to any other Character of their choice.
f) The rest of the Party continues the game.
• From now on, only Characters remaining in play are counted when effects check their number icon.
g) In the meantime, the player without a Character may create a new Character following the “Re-joining an Adventure in Progress” instructions in Chapter 2 of the Player’s Handbook. The new Character will join the Party during the next Camp Phase.
h) In the very rare case where all Characters die before the Camp Sheet resolves, your Adventure is unfortunately over. Reset your game, create new Characters, and start over!

TERROR icon

Terror represents a Character’s mental fatigue that results from fear, effects of chaotic magic, or witnessing gruesome scenes.
Each Character has 4 Boons&Terror slots on their Character board. Received Terror tokens are placed in these slots and can be mitigated with various effects, similarly to damage.

PART 4: DETAILED RULES PARTY

A Party Member is a term that refers to all Characters and all Companions in play. Party Members may form one or several Parties, depending on their location. Party Members are in one Party whenever:

  • There is no physical representation of the Party or the Characters in play.
  • They are represented by the Party model.
  • They are represented by separate Character models, but share the same space on the current Sheet of the Adventure.

In Combat, Characters always occupy different spaces. That is why they do not follow Party rules, even though they are still referred to as Party Members.

EXPLORATION ENTRIES AND STORYBOOK NAVIGATION

A Storybook contains numbered entries for your current Adventure. The Sheets, Actions, rules, and cards of the Adventure often refer to these entries. All entries are sorted in ascending order, though not all entry numbers are used.

icon Nightbloom Crossing 160

This icon followed by a number shows that the appropriate Storybook entry might be read in this space. This usually requires performing an Action from the Secret Sheet.

REDIRECTIONS

A Storybook will frequently instruct you to go to different entries depending on keywords and marks that you have or choices the Party makes. Whenever you find a redirection with a requirement that you meet (this might be a keyword, Item, mark or anything else), you must follow this redirection immediately – even if only one person in the Party has it – and disregard any other text below the redirection.

Example:

  • If you have Dwerg or Warfare, go to 9.
  • If you have Battle Magic, go to 10.

You check the tokens in the Character and Items spaces of your board. You notice that your Profession token has a Warfare keyword, while your other Party Member has a Battle Magic keyword. This means you have to go to entry 9. The Battle Magic redirection does not take place – since your Party meets the criteria of the first redirection, you are not allowed to read or resolve anything beyond it.

CHOICES

In many places, a Storybook will give you multiple choices. These always start with the word "choose". You may read all the text in each choice before committing to one. However, if the outcomes redirect to any additional entries, you may not go there before you make your decision.

If a choice has a requirement, your Party needs to match this requirement to make that choice. It is enough for one Party Member to fulfill the requirement!

Example:

Choose:

  • (Requires Dwerg or Scholarship) Speak to them in Dwergish – go to 100.
  • Tell them about the bandits – You hope to convince them to help your cause. Go to 101.
  • Return to the main square – go to 95.

Unless the Character who makes the choice is specified, the choice belongs to the entire Party. The Party can only pick one of the available options. If you can't come to an agreement, you should discuss and try to convince each other which decision makes the most sense. If you still can't resolve the situation, you should hold a vote about which option to choose. If the vote is tied, the owner of the Decision token has to use it to break the stalemate. After they do that, they have to pass the Decision token to the next player clockwise.

RESTRICTED PARAGRAPHS

In some parts of a Storybook, you will find paragraphs that have separate requirements you need to match to read them. Sometimes those restrictions may refer to single Characters, who may read the paragraph following the restriction.

Example:

  • If you have Dwerg: Knowing the dwergish customs you explain that (...).

This part might be read by the whole Party if any of the Characters have the Dwerg keyword.

  • Characters with Medicine: There is something off about the body (...).

This part may be read only by Characters that meet the requirement and is not read out loud for the entire Party. The player may share the information with the rest of the Party or keep it as a secret to himself.

MARKS, STORY TRACKER AND TIME TOKEN

The Story Tracker is used to track the consequences of your choices. When an effect tells you to mark a slot in the Story Tracker (A-Z), place a marker in the slot with the letter mentioned in the effect. If you have to mark a slot that is already marked, ignore this effect.

The bottom of the Story Tracker has a round space for a Time token – it may count hours or days, and sometimes even more abstract things. The rules and setup of specific Time tokens are described in the Adventures that use them. The current state of the Time token is shown by the arrow on the left of the slot.

If you have to progress the Time token, rotate it clockwise. If a Time token is reduced, rotate it anti-clockwise.

a) This Character takes a number of D12 dice equal to their Attribute (ATT) matching the one in the Roll.

ROLL MODIFIERS

Most Roll modifiers are positive, providing additional dice or lowering the Difficulty Level ( icon ). However, some are also negative. When applying them, follow these rules:

  1. The Character performing the Roll has to apply every modifier for which they meet any of the listed conditions.

  2. Apply each modifier once, even if the Character meets more than one of these conditions.
    Note: Even when you have several different keywords that enable one modifier, you do not apply this modifier several times!

  3. If the Character meets the same condition multiple times, apply the modifier the same number of times. This means having two instances of the same keyword allows you to apply that modifier twice.

  4. Modifiers can never reduce the number of dice rolled below 1 or reduce the icon below 2.

Example:
A Dwerg Character who has 3 CHA and keywords Cityborn, Charm, Charm, and Diplomacy performs the following Roll:
Player with Cityborn, Deception or Merchant Rolls for CHA icon +1 die: Charm, Diplomacy, Midlands, -1 die: Dwerg
After applying all modifiers, they roll 4 dice:

  • 3 for CHA Attribute.

  • +2 for meeting conditions – +1 for each Charm they have.
    They cannot benefit from both Charm and Diplomacy at the same time, so they choose Charm as it gives them a better bonus.

  • -1 for meeting a Dwerg condition.

SUPPORT

Supporting means to roll additional dice to help another Character when they perform a Roll. The following rules apply to Exploration. For other situations see "Supporting in Combat" (p. 23) or "Camp Sheet & Camp Phase" (p. 19). Companions may also aid in Rolls (see "Companions" p. 26).
If Characters are in a Party, and one of them is performing a Roll, all other Characters who meet the conditions of at least one modifier may Support them, according to the rules below:

  • Each Supporting Character rolls only the dice they receive from modifiers.
  • If a Supporting Character meets more than one modifier condition, they must still choose a single keyword to apply the modifier.
  • Modifiers with conditions that are not related to keywords can only be applied by the Character who performs the Roll, and never by the Supporting Characters.
  • Any Successes rolled are added to the performing Character's Roll.
  • Any other effects related to the dice values rolled by the Supporting Characters, such as gaining Fate, are resolved by the Supporting Character.

Example:
Characters Alice, Beth, and Charlie are in a Party and resolve the following Roll:
Find and disarm traps – (...) Roll for INT icon , +2 dice: icon , +1 die: Subterranean, Engineering, Sleight of Hand.

  • Slot H is marked in the Story Tracker.
  • Alice has 2 INT and the Engineering keyword.
  • Beth has no keywords that match the conditions.

  • Charlie has the Sleight of Hand keyword twice.
    Players decide that Alice will perform this Roll:

  • Alice rolls 5 dice: 2 for INT, 2 for mark H in the Story Tracker and 1 for Engineering.

  • Beth cannot Support.
  • Charlie Supports with 2 dice, for a double keyword.
    The Roll has Difficulty 10 and the dice results are:
    Alice: 12, 9, 7, 3.
    Charlie: 10, 1.
    The total result is 3 Successes: 2 for a natural 12 and an additional 1 from the Support. Charlie also gains 1 Fate for rolling a natural 1.
DIVINUM IN ROLLS

The Character making the Roll adds a Divinum die indicated by the Divinum value on their board to the pool of D12 dice. Each Roll that uses a DIV die has its own rules for its use, usually specified in the Roll's outcome and usually uses icons as the full value icon or half of the value icon rolled.
DIV dice do not count toward determining the number of Successes or gaining Fate. Some game effects may allow you to add more DIV dice to the roll. In such cases, the DIV results are added together.

MARKERS, DANGER AND QUEST MODELS

Storybook or Secret Sheet Actions often place, count or refer to markers somewhere in the game area. CAMP SHEET & CAMP PHASE

Characters rest on a Camp Sheet – a special type of a Secret Sheet with holders for Camp cards. Each slot in the Camp Sheet is marked with a letter (A-H) – Camp cards can only be slotted in holders with corresponding letters. Camp allows the Party to regain HP, reorganize their Equipment, Advance their Characters, and interact with their Companions.

Whenever the game asks you to resolve a Camp Phase, place the Camp Sheet in the available space of the Story Tracker, and follow the procedure listed on the Sheet. Here is an overview of the Camp Phase:

  • Step 1. Performing Advancements – Characters with Advancement tokens in their Pouches may spend them to gain new cards and abilities from their Profession or Secondary Advancement Trees (see "Chapter 9" in the Player's Handbook).
  • Step 2. Rest – Each Party Member heals all HP. Each Character refreshes all their Action cards ignoring all costs and restrictions.
  • Step 3. Recquip – Characters may exchange Items and Companions. Then, they can equip items from their Pouches and change Action cards on their Hotbar.
  • Steps 4&5. Expand and upgrade Camp – The Party may add new Camp cards or improve the current ones (with entries marked as P found in the Player's Handbook).
  • Step 6. The Party may recruit new Companions.
  • Step 7. The Party may replace Camp cards. Place unused Camp cards in the Party Inventory.
  • Steps 8&9. Resolve Camp Actions – Each Character may resolve one Action from any Camp card. Place the Character models on the cards to indicate what they are doing. Only Characters on the same Camp card may Support another one's Rolls.
  • Step 10. Clean up – Discard the Camp Sheet along with all models and markers on it.

Once the Camp Phase is complete, return to the same place that asked you to resolve the Camp Phase (an entry, a card, or a Sheet), and continue the game.

COMBAT

Each time you encounter a "Start the Combat" instruction in a Storybook entry, resolve the Combat according to the rules below. Some rules, like performing Rolls, are similar to the Exploration part of the Adventure.

Combat is played in Rounds, each divided into Activations of Characters and Enemies. The Round ends after all Party Members and Enemies perform their Activation. Over the course of a Round, each Character and each Enemy will become Activated. The order is decided by drawing tokens from the Initiative bag.

Activated Characters perform two Actions (see "Character Activation" p. 22). Activated Enemies first resolve the rules on their Enemy card, and then the top Activation card from their Activation deck. After the Activation, draw another token and resolve another Activation to continue the Combat.

COMBAT SETUP

The setup of each Combat is described in the Storybook. A Combat setup will typically prepare the following components:

  • Secret Sheet with grid spaces.
  • Initiative bag and Activation tokens.
  • Enemy cards that form a row (the card on the left will be Activated first).
  • Enemy Activation cards that form small face-down decks under some Enemies.
  • Enemy models.
  • Character models.
ROUND STRUCTURE

The Initiative bag determines the order of Activations in Combat. Characters and Enemies Activate one by one according to the drawn tokens.

The initial number of Activation tokens is always determined by the Combat setup in the Storybook. However, various game effects can add tokens to the bag.

To determine whether a Character or an Enemy is Activated next, draw a token from the Initiative bag. Depending on the type of token drawn, resolve the effects below:

  • White token – The Party decides together which non-activated Character to Activate. Place the token on their Character board and resolve their Activation.
  • Black token – Place the token on the first Enemy card from the left without an Activation token and resolve its Activation.
  • Dual token – Count the number of white and black Activation tokens already drawn from the bag. If there are more black tokens, treat this token as white. Otherwise, if there are more white tokens (or there is a tie), treat it as a black token.

The Round ends after all Party Members or all Enemies are fully Activated. This means either all Enemies have a black token on their cards or all Characters have a white token on their boards. When that happens, resolve all remaining Activations as specified:

  • If any Enemy cards have not been Activated yet, Activate them, going from left to right.
  • If any Characters have not been Activated yet, Activate them in the order chosen by players.

Then, return all Activation tokens from Characters and Enemies to the Initiative bag. Start the next round.

Note: In a rare case, when there are no more tokens in the Initiative bag to draw, resolve all remaining Enemy Activations from left to right and then all Character Activations in a chosen order.

VICTORY & DEFEAT

Combat continues until the Objective listed on the Sheet is achieved (causing you to resolve the Victory entry) or the Party is Defeated (see below). Achieving Victory instantly ends the Combat, including the currently resolved Action.

The Party is Defeated when all Characters are Knocked Out or Eliminated (in other words, each of the Characters has 0 HP). In such a case, immediately resolve the Defeat entry of your current Sheet.

A Partner in solo mode counts as a Character for this purpose, but other Companions do not.

KNOCKED OUT & ELIMINATION

If a Character is Knocked Out, they cannot further participate in Combat, until they get Stabilized. They gain 1 Fate and 1 Injury Condition. Discard their model from the Secret Sheet along with all Effect tokens of that Character, and other Effect tokens affecting this Character. Place one Effect token of that Character in the space where their model was. Other Characters may target this Effect token with Actions that Stabilize (e.g. First Aid, see "Basic Actions" p. 23). Other than that, this Character is immune to any effects.

Some game effects may Eliminate a Character from Combat instead – if that happens, reduce their HP to 0, but they do not gain an Injury unless stated otherwise. They cannot be brought back during this Combat and their Effect token is not placed on the Secret Sheet (or it is discarded if it is already there). After the Combat, the Character who was Knocked Out or Eliminated rolls their DIV and restores a number of HP equal to the result. If the Knocked Out or Elimination happens during a Character's Action, that Action is interrupted.

Enemy Activation cards determine the Actions taken by Enemies during Combat. Each card contains several numbered Actions that the Enemy performs in their Activation. Each Action may target a different Party Member (see "Targeting" below). If an Enemy Action cannot be fulfilled, resolve as much of it as possible.

Enemies do not make any attack Rolls – they deal a set amount of damage. However, your Character can use Reactions to defend against attacks.

TARGETING

Enemy Actions will target Characters, spaces on the Sheet, or other components. If targeting conditions are met by more than one target, players choose the outcome. The most common targeting conditions are:

  • icon – The Enemy targets the closest Party Member (regardless of cover).
  • icon or icon – The Enemy targets the Party Member with the highest number of targeting icons present on Action cards in the Hotbar. Both the front and back of a card may contain those symbols, but only the currently visible side contributes its icons toward Enemy targeting.

An Enemy can only target Characters or objects that are in range of their Action. If there is no valid target, the Action is skipped.

Example:
An Enemy is resolving their Activation. The current Action says:
Move 3 toward icon.
There are 3 Characters in Combat:
• Alice has 3 icon icons but due to Impassable borders, there is no possible path for the Enemy to reach them.
• Beth has 1 icon and is in range 1 from the Enemy.
• Charlie has 1 icon and is in range 1 from the Enemy.
The Enemy ignores Alice, even though they have the most icon.
Since there are two possible targets, and they are tied on the number of icon, the players may decide whether Beth or Charlie will become the target of this Action, regardless of the range they are in from the Enemy.

Enemies may target multiple Party Members with a single Action. In such a case target a set number of Party Members starting with the ones who have the most targeting icons.

ENEMY MOVEMENT

Enemies do not have a set movement value. Instead, they move a number of spaces written on the Activation card. When moving, an Enemy follows a set of rules:

  • Enemies cannot move through Impassable borders.
  • Enemies do not move through Dangerous spaces if entering them would kill that Enemy.
  • Enemies cannot move through spaces occupied by Party Members (Characters, Companions, etc).
  • Enemies can move through spaces occupied by other Enemies, but they cannot end their movement in a space occupied by an Enemy.

When an Enemy moves toward something, each space has to get them closer to the target. When moving away, the Enemy has to end their movement in a space further from the target than the one it started in.

ENEMY ATTACKS

All Enemy Actions that have the Attack keyword are considered attacks. They may come with different keywords like Melee, Ranged and/or Spell. Those keywords do not have an inherent function, but might be checked by other effects. Each Enemy attack specifies its own range and targets. Characters may use their Defensive Reactions against Enemy attacks. If not specified, Enemies have unlimited range for their Actions and attacks.

AREA OF EFFECT ATTACKS

Some Enemy Actions are displayed in the form of a graphic diagram. The diagram defines the area of the Action and the effects. These effects affect both Characters and other Enemies.
This means that, for example, a Fireball from an enemy Mage may kill some of his own allies if they are present in the affected spaces.

ENEMY HP AND DEFEATING ENEMIES

The HP of Enemies is written on their card. Minions always have 1 HP each, and HP for Boss and Elite Enemies is tracked on their HP dials. To defeat an Enemy, you must deal enough damage to reduce their HP to 0. When this happens, remove the Enemy's model from the Secret Sheet and flip their card. If the Enemy is represented with more than one model, flip its card only after you have removed the last model for that Enemy. When the Enemy card is flipped, discarded or changed during Combat, discard all components from it (markers, Effect tokens, etc.), except the Activation token if this Enemy has already Activated this round. From this moment on, that Enemy does not take part in Combat – it cannot be targeted by any effects. Enemies with their cards flipped may still resolve some effects. If the card states "Do not Activate this Enemy" the card is fully skipped in the Initiative order. Some Enemy cards have a formula that calculates their starting HP based on the number of Characters (icon or icon). Enemies may heal above their starting HP.
Note: In some late game Combats, while playing 3+ Characters, the Enemy HP may exceed 100. To mark it use the 100/200 HP token.

CHARACTER ACTIVATION

Each time a white token is drawn from the Initiative bag, one of the Characters will Activate. At the beginning of the Activation, you refresh all their Action cards – flip them from the dark side to the light side. Some Action cards may have additional conditions or costs to be refreshed. During the Activation, resolve 2 Actions – you may spend them on Basic Actions, Action cards or Resolve Actions. Some Actions do not count against this limit – they are called "free Actions".

BASIC ACTIONS

Each Character has access to Basic Actions – Move and First Aid. They are listed on the Help card, but here is the breakdown of how they work.

MOVE

When you use a Move Action, move your model up to a number of spaces equal to your MOB value.

  • You can only move to connected spaces (not diagonally).
  • You cannot cross Impassable borders.
  • You cannot move through spaces with Enemy models.
  • You may go through spaces with Party Members, but cannot end movement on an occupied space.

ACTION CARDS

Action cards are attacks, tricks, combat maneuvers and spells that a Character has access to. They can only be used during Combat. Action cards have two sides – light when they are available to use and dark when they are unavailable. The dark side may contain passive effects following the Action, refresh conditions and costs, or in rare cases, even another Action that can be made.

Action cards are divided into six types: Offensive Actions, Offensive Reactions, Defensive Actions, Defensive Reactions, Support Actions and Support Reactions. The type is indicated by an appropriate tag on the left side of the card. Cards have to be stored in slots of a matching type on the Hotbar to be used. After using an Action card, flip it to the dark side (unless stated otherwise).

Cards may be used in one of two possible moments:

  • As an Action – A Character has to spend one Action to use this card. Usually, those cards instruct the player to perform a Roll. The number of Successes may determine how much damage will be dealt or if any additional effects will be triggered. The difficulty of the Roll may either be determined by the Roll itself or by the Enemy Difficulty icon (see “Enemies” p. 21).
  • As a Reaction – A Character may use a Reaction in any moment specified by the card.

Characters may use a Reaction if the timing allows for it. Each Character may use any number of Reactions against a single effect. Defensive Reactions are an exception to that – only one can be used against each attack (see the following example).

Example: Alice is being attacked and uses a “Dodge” Defensive Reaction card. This Reaction requires a Roll. Both Beth and Charlie may use their “Combat Insight” Support Reaction cards to Support Alice in that Roll. Alice fails the Roll and they cannot use another Defensive Reaction like “Endurance” to try to reduce the incoming damage again.

Offensive Actions like attacks gain the keywords from the item that is required to use them (e.g. Powerful Shot requires a Ranged keyword and can be used with a Bow Item).

ACTION CARDS TARGETING

Each Action card specifies its possible targets. Usually it is a number of Characters or Enemies in a set range. The diagram in the top right corner of the card serves as a reminder and visual representation of what can be targeted. Targeting specifies the number of targets and range. It is often written in short forms, such as: CH (Character), PM (Party Member), Self (the Character using this Action), EN (Enemy), Minion, Elite, Boss, All (both Party Members and Enemies) and others.

REFRESHING ACTION CARDS

Refreshing a card means to flip it from the dark side to the light side. Action cards refresh at the beginning of an Activation, unless the red text on the card says otherwise. Some Actions (mostly Defensive Reactions) might be additionally refreshed at any time by paying the Fate listed on the card. Other cards might have different costs and moments to be refreshed – all written in blue.

SUPPORTING IN COMBAT

During Combat, Characters cannot Support each other the same way they do during Exploration. In Combat, each time a Party Member performs a Roll, other Party Members may use one of their Reactions to Support the Roll. The Reaction must allow you to do so, and all requirements of range have to be fulfilled. The main way to Support each other’s Rolls is with the “Combat Insight” Reaction cards.

RANGE AND COVER RANGE

Many rules and cards require you to count spaces or check if the target is in range, measured in spaces. These spaces can be counted through Impassable borders. Below are the most common terms related to range, with explanations.

Connected space – any space that shares a Passable border with your space. Diagonal squares in a square grid are not connected.

Spaces with numbers 1 and 3 are connected to each other.
Spaces with numbers 1 and 4 are not.

Range (R#) – describes how far the target of the effect may be from the source of the effect. The game does not use the concept of line of sight – it is assumed that all models see one another, with range being the most common restriction when determining if the Action may or may not target something. All spaces up to the mentioned number are within range – this means R1 is included in R2 and so on.

These four red spaces are in R1 of the Character.
All red spaces are in R2 from the Character.
All red spaces are in R2+ from the Character.

In line – an effect which originates from a Character or Enemy and may affect targets in any space in a line of connected spaces. Effects may include: dealing icon, moving and so on.

In line effect may target anything in the red spaces.

COVER

Characters and Enemies can use terrain in Combat to gain Cover and protect themselves better against attacks and other hostile effects. When performing an attack that targets a model in Cover, that model gets +2 armor against this attack.

A model has Cover if it is in a space with an Impassable border. This Cover bonus applies against all damage dealt by anyone on the other side of this Impassable border (see example below).

Example: The Character (on the left) has Cover against Enemies on any spaces marked with yellow, including Enemy 1, but does not have Cover against Enemy 2. These Enemies do not benefit from Cover when the Character targets them, as they are not next to any Impassable borders. If Enemy 1 damages the Character, they gets +2 armor against this attack.

Example: For a large Enemy to benefit from Cover, all spaces from one side of the model have to have an Impassable border.

EFFECT TOKENS

Some Action tokens use Effect tokens to mark ongoing effects that affect the game. Effect tokens can be placed on both Secret Sheet spaces and on Character portraits and Enemy cards participating in Combat. Effect tokens do not block spaces that they occupy.

  • If the effect says to place an Effect token on a Character, Companion or Enemy, place the token on the appropriate portrait or card. Usually the Action that placed the Effect token will specify how it works in the game.
  • Each Character has access to 8 Effect tokens in their color with two different icons.
  • One token has to be placed on a card that is the source of the effect and the other tokens are used to mark what is affected by it.
  • A Character may have up to 2 effects that use Effect tokens in play at any given moment. If they were to have a third, they may discard all Effect tokens with one icon first to accommodate it.
  • Enemies may also use Effect tokens – they are marked with a square.
SHEET ACTIONS DURING COMBAT

Some Combat Secret Sheets may include additional tools and Actions for Party Members to use. They come in three different types:

  • Printed Action cards – Party Members may use those Actions as if they were on their Hotbar. They are not flipped after use, but might be restricted to a single use per Activation.
  • Additional Action cards – similar to standard Action cards, but placed next to the Secret Sheet. Party Members may use them as if they were on their Hotbars. They are flipped after use.
  • Non-card Sheet Actions – additional, other Actions. Party Members may spend one of their Actions during Activation to resolve them, similarly to a Basic Action.

ARMOR

Both Characters and Enemies may have armor. Whenever damage icon is dealt to a Party Member or Enemy, reduce that amount by their armor icon. If there are multiple effects of armor for a single Character or Enemy, they add together.

There may also be effects that allow the attacker to ignore the armor of the attacked model. In such a case, temporarily reduce the armor value by the value of the Ignore Armor effect. This may reduce the value down to 0.

LOSING HP

Some effects in the game may instruct Characters or Enemies to lose a set number of HP instead of dealing icon – this will be marked as “lose HP” and will disregard armor.

OPTIONAL RULES SIGNALIZED ACTIVATIONS

To make Combat less random, you can reveal one Enemy Activation card for each Enemy at the start of each Round, instead of when that Enemy Activates. This change will make Combats more predictable and take longer to play.

MEANINGFUL AND CRITICAL FAILURES

To increase the impact of Failure outcomes in Rolls, you can use any combination of the following rules:

a) "1" dice results only give Fate if you fail your entire Roll.

b) Each "1" result subtracts 1 Success from your Roll. If this would cause the number of Successes to decrease below 0, treat the outcome as 0 Successes.

Remember that these rules, especially when used together, affect the Fate token economy and increase the difficulty of the game.

FRIENDLY FIRE

Players may decide to include the rule that all area-of-effects hit all models present in the affected area regardless if it's a Character, Enemy, or Companion.

Or for the sake of immersion players may decide each time if the effect should or should not hit other combatants.

COMPANIONS

To make the game more difficult, if your Party has any Companions, treat your Character Number as 1 higher.
